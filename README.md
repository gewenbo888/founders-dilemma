# The Founder's Dilemma · 开国者的两难

A bilingual comparative atlas of how China's founding rulers treated the meritorious
generals who won them the empire — and a darker lens on the circumstance hidden inside virtue.

> 鸟尽弓藏，兔死狗烹。
> When the birds are gone, the good bow is stored away; when the cunning rabbit is
> dead, the hunting hound goes into the pot. — Han Xin, on being bound for execution.

Six founders, side by side: **Liu Bang** (killed the kings, kept the clerks), **Liu Xiu**
(the cleanest record — and the luckiest), **Li Shimin** (merciful to officials, merciless
to blood), **Zhao Kuangyin** (bought the swords back with wine), **Zhu Yuanzhang** (burned
the ladder he climbed), and the **modern marshals** (persecution, then rehabilitation).

The central thesis, drawn from the source conversation: **who dies first** often decides
whether a ruler's virtue is ever tested. Liu Xiu's rival brother was removed early by
another man's hand, so he never had to choose; Li Shimin's rivals were alive at the
decisive moment, so we remember both the massacre and the golden age. *Untested virtue is
not the same thing as demonstrated restraint.*

## Sections

1. **The frame** — why founders kill the men who crowned them
2. **Six founders** — case-study cards with two mercy meters and a one-line verdict
3. **Two kinds of mercy** — interactive 2D matrix (mercy to officials × mercy to kin)
4. **Who dies first** — the thesis, told as two parallel succession timelines
5. **Loyalty is a situation** — Han Xin, Han Wang Xin, Lu Wan: the "traitors" who weren't
